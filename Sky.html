The Red Valley Sky


Driving down highway 395 Tom sat dead still contemplating the machinery of thought. His body a ventriloquist dummy, right hand shifting gears as if possessed by the god of intuition. “He not busy being born is busy dying”, Bob Dylan blared from Tom’s weak dysfunctional speakers. Tom’s eyes were freshly butchered ribeye’s, marbled with tinged echoes of madness and interspersed with striated cracks of hope. His pupils were dilated as if his face had been torn off and replaced with that of a poorly sewn owls. Death Valley was a long shot eight ball racking Tom’s thoughts, a shivering bet of redemption. Three sheets of acid and a six pack were all Tom cared about. A tent and near empty campfire stove were mere necessities. A Colt Defender lay dormant in his glove box, an abstract meditation of infinite possibility.

Tom sat in his cubicle pleasantly bashing away on his trusty keyboard, a dear friend of whose feelings he understood clear as the Utah sky. Spitting out functions like a broccoli fed toddler, Tom gained steam and blessed the holy screen with entrancing productivity, hoarding host and guzzling the blood of Christ, hypnotizing the choir and finessing the pipe organ with singular virtuosity. Lunch was out of the question, Tom was on a mission. Tom’s firm dealt with financial analytics, requiring Tom to manage the security of monetary transactions. Tom had as dull and philosophically distant job as one could painfully imagine, Jung careening backward from his sweet little light. Although his purpose was easily dismissible in the grand chaos of life, he was endlessly pleased to contribute to the structure of unnatural order. Although Tom was happily engrossed in his work, he was exceptionally excited at the prospect of proposing to his lovely girlfriend Fiona, the second place unhealthy obsession controlling the diaspora of his meaningless existence. This despicable display of unwarranted positivity drove Fiona insane, her inevitable onset of dementia, reducing the timeline of her peaceful vitality. Fiona hated Tom with a creative passion. Thoughts of unlit carbon monoxide garages and drunken freight train nightmares provoked hypnotic sea-foam dreams and exacerbated a deep concentration of the borderline psychotic American dream. Fiona taught preschool. She despised her job. Tom sat complacent and stiff behind the endlessly rotating steering wheel. Left, right, one, zero, on, off, the means to an end with a brave beginning. Tom’s mind was a blizzard of fearless energy. He imagined watching Neil Armstrong blow his brains out while planting the American flag in the depths of our Nations television existence.

Clouds crept by effortlessly as Tom stopped to take a piss by a sad looking cactus, sagging needles like a broke desperate junkies daydream. Tom sighed pleasantly as he watered the thirsty cactus. “Gotta keep those needles sharp boy”. Tom was a delirious shit show, a shell of past hope, a prizeless Cadbury egg. Tom snapped off a healthy needle and calmly pricked his thumb, a drop of blood rising and falling to the cracked desert earth. “Life’s a peach”, Tom said to the cactus. There was no reply.

Fiona walked defeatedly down the constricting preschool hall, one kid skipping by in a blur, her pigtails swaying in unison. Fiona cracked a weak, sincere smile of pain. A tear slid effortlessly down her cheek, falling to the pavement and evaporating instantly. The small pool of depression devoured by heat. She opened the door of her beat up Honda civic and sighed a warm breath of loss. “Life’s not what I pictured, but Christ if it couldn’t be worse,” Fiona thought as she started her car, the rusted motor churning like the knots in her stomach. Fiona was good, Tom was good, conflicting points of view brought war and psychology. Without the hurt, humanity would flat line, children would cease to exist in their wise little bubbles of hope, and stillness would envelop the land. Noise wouldn’t matter a blink. Noise wouldn’t exist without life. Fiona’s slot machine thoughts faded to static as she turned on the radio and tuned out.

Tom drove fast, his teeth clenched, eyes bulging while he tightly gripped the jet black steering wheel. His thumb was throbbing, he gripped the wheel harder. “Who in his realms of love, who in his mirror,” Tom loved a good dirge. Nervous echoes of past shadows brought down with ramshackle poetic force. He sipped a yoohoo he’d bought at the only seventy-six for miles, tank full, stomach empty. The sweet pseudo chocolate felt cool and strange on Tom’s tongue. The engine stuttered as Tom passed the last semblance of civilization before a dead straight shot of endless desert highway. It was an old weathered house, shattered windows and peeling white exterior. It seemed as though somebody built it, only to run screaming on completion. “It must be haunted,” Tom thought. He was correct, but like a well-planned homicide, nobody would ever find out. Infinite vacancy, entropy working its mysterious beauty. Tom took another sip of yoohoo, grimacing at the thick sweet sickly contents. He tossed the bottle out the window. Tom smiled at the faint sound of shattering glass. That yoohoo never stood a chance. Tom hit the gas and felt the rapid burst of increased speed in his ageing gut. He watched an oasis in the distance, miraculous pyramids of genius design, tiny figures soaked in blood, sherry red slaves being whipped like horses as they placed one brick atop the next, masons of the past fearing their pharaoh god. Moses could be their children’s salvation, and their sinking lineages death sentence. A single olive execution.

Fiona sat calmly in her apartment eating cold sub-par chow mien from the local greasy chino joint. “Chow Funk, what a god awful name,” Fiona said aloud to her fat grey cat Horace. She ate measuredly, being very careful not to drop the slightest bit of food. Her manners were impeccable, her family made sure of it. Fiona’s dad was a raving alcoholic, he’d recently passed. When the phone rang with the grieving sobs of her sweet little mother’s voice, she’d hung up and laughed so hard her ritz and peanut butter snack became lodged tightly in her esophagus and forced her to run to her neighbor’s for help. He squeezed firmly on her chest, the peanut butter snack soaring through the air, slow motion beauty painting the wall of her savior’s apartment. They’d slept together several times a week after that. Tom was none the wiser. Fiona dropped a noodle on the floor. Horace chomped on it gratefully. Fiona went into her bathroom and stared in the mirror. Her brown eyes shimmered with a universal wisdom, spiraling galaxies of a woman’s knowledge far beyond anyone Fiona’s age had any right to possess. “A curse,” she thought. It was a blessing. Fiona was fairly pretty, a tall thin brunette, sharp angular features that excited a tone of authority and purpose. She raised the small stick, the tiny blue line racking her mind with hate. She’d done the abortion deal. That awful, hot little room, wide eyed men with epileptic knees, calm young ladies like handcuffed convicts, knowing and accepting their plight while sad pale boyfriends cried hysterically with joy and discomfort in their selfish one note heads. It was the neighbor’s kid. Tom never stood a chance.

Tom was unraveling, his instability rising. He saw a man on the side of the road kneeling low and inspecting a flat on the rear left tire of his pathetic looking Toyota pickup. “Fucking chink mobile,” Tom said, drooling and grinding his teeth. His eyes were pink and wild. He hadn’t slept for days. A manic man with no past and no hope in hell of a decent future. Tom slowed to a crawl and rolled down his window. “Need a hand there?” Tom asked grinning far too wide. He was in dire need of a Manchester smile. Tom licked his lips at the thought. “That’d sure be kind of ye,” the man said breathlessly. He had a patchy black mustache and beat up straw bent cowboy hat and was, contrary to Tom’s racist ramblings, white as a snowflake. Tom narrowed his eyes and studied the man with an acute awareness of the lonely desert highway stretching in all directions toward an infinity of empty horizon. Tom reached instinctively into his glove box and extracted the Colt Defender. He tucked it in his belt, a child playing sheriff. Tom rolled up the window and stepped out of the car. He slowly approached the harmless man. “What can I help ya with there bud?”

“Gee, this is mighty kind of you, if ya can just hold this wrench while I jack up this shit brick tire I just bought. Them snakes is drillin oil from us trustin folks, names Jim.” He extended a warm palm toward Tom. “Don’t tell me you’re fucking name you little backwards prick,” Tom whispered, ears steaming. “What was that?” the man asked frowning. “Nothin at all, just thinkin is all, names Tom.” They shook hands cordially as Jim produced a beat up wrench. He handed it to Tom and turned back toward his vehicle. Tom watched him hypnotized, barely registering the man’s scrambled, deep coded words vibrating meaninglessly against his skull. “Been workin at this for ages. Aint no mechanic, but I get by just fine. Old Brenda here’s been good to Jim.” Tom caught the last bit, shivering at the notion of a man referring to himself in the third person. “You don’t say,” said Tom, teeth ground straight like a horse. Tom raised the wrench high and brought it down like a whip against the back of the man’s shoulder, the sickly crack of bone slicing through the white hot desert heat. Jim screamed and gasped, rolling on his side and shaking violently as he clutched his broken collar bone. “FUCK, WHY, JESUS MAN!” Jim was crying in shock and pain. “Pathetic little beetle, sucking up the air of the civilized,” Tom thought with true, unflinching belief. He pulled out his Colt Defender and aimed it at the man’s right leg. Cocking the gun, Tom pulled the trigger bullseye on Jim’s shin. Jim screamed louder, helpless, unearthly sounds as blood sprayed from his leg, tagging his car with flecks of plasma and splintered shinbone. Tom cocked the gun again and fired into Jim’s broken collar bone. Jim’s eyes rolled back in his head and he lay still as he struggled for breath. Tom cocked the gun one last time and pressed it to Jim’s right eye. “Which layer Jim?” Tom asked quietly. “Please, oh Jesus I have a son, I have a family…”

“I said which fuckin layer Jim,” Tom said, his voice growing impatient. “Please, please don’t.” Tom pulled the trigger firmly. Jim’s face disappeared in a red mess of violence as brain and skull painted his tire. Jim lay peacefully, half his face caved in, blood spurting in short pathetic bursts. “You bastard. You had your pick of hell,” Tom said as he unloaded randomly on Jim’s corpse. Tom thought briefly, rolled Jim’s small body under the car, and swiftly kicked the jack. The truck came down fast, crushing Jim’s body, bones snapping, tendons shredding. Tom smiled wide as he stepped into his car. Nobody deserved a Manchester smile more than broken old Tom.

Fiona awoke from her nap in a haze of smoky nightmares. Fractured visions of dying infants lying in fields of buckwheat. She walked disoriented to her bathroom, showered and brushed her teeth. Fiona stepped into her kitchenette and pulled out a bottle of blue label from her cupboard. She couldn’t remember where it had come from, some long past, forgotten gift. She poured a stiff thumb into an old tumbler with a picture of Charlie Chaplin adorning its side. It had been her fathers. She had no idea why she’d held on to the glass.  Some prototype of love resided deep down in the caverns of her psyche. Fiona sipped the drink slowly as she contemplated her childhood. Being an only child, Fiona took solace in the warm, cheerful embrace of her mother, kissing her on the cheek and grinning, her kind face with its swollen purple right eye and glowing demeanor. Fiona finished her scotch and walked out of her door, down the stairs, and out of her complex into the cool evening air.

Tom aimed true and shot at a star. "Damn, just missed it." He'd lost focus and aimed a hair left. The desert had cooled, the weighted air of day shifting with polarity to a weightless night. The sky was infested with stars, an alien fireworks show, the finally of some divine lunar scape. Cutting his losses and mindful of the degrading pile of loose bullets resting idly in his glove box, Tom walked defeatedly to his car and cracked open a beer. He drank half and sat in the driver’s seat. Cold sweat hung frightened on the tip of his chin. Tom looked ahead with sharp focus, something was moving slowly through the desert. A tall, black-coffee figure moving gracefully through the landscape. A deer moved into view of Tom's weak headlights. Something was off. The deer was shuffling across the highway on its hind legs with jerky, measured purpose. The deer slowed its movement and stopped dead in front of Tom's car, slowly turning its head and staring straight at him. The deer's eyes were a searing indigo fire, far too human esque, quiet orbs of torture. The creature peered into Tom's soul. Tom was numb and thoughtless as he watched in wonder. His thoughts awoke and swayed gently toward a straight- razor mistake as the creature smiled wide at Tom, winking knowingly before turning and shuffling away past a cactus and into the temperate desert night.

Fiona drove purposefully, frowning slightly, her delicate eyelashes curled beautifully as she staunchly followed every rule of traffic, glancing occasionally at her speedometer. Fiona felt empowered at the prospect of ditching Tom. Their date was planned entirely around a quick and painless escape. Cars rushed by aimlessly, a stark contrast to her lawful precision. Fiona approached a seventy-six and sat patiently while gas streamed into her tank. The young attendant stood idly, watching Fiona with a forlorn expression while a small lady screamed gibberish at some invisible presence behind him. Feeling thoroughly uncomfortable by this point, Fiona removed the pump and opened her door. "Be careful,” the attendant said quietly. Fiona looked at him raising an eyebrow. "What was that?" The attendant stared at her in silence, the woman behind him now speaking to a full on jury. The restaurant was fairly close to the seventy-six. Fiona pulled into the parking lot and shut off the engine, her confidence momentarily waned.

Tom’s right boot provoked a minute cloud of dust like a Hiroshima diorama as he stepped out of his car. The rising sun projected a mosaic of naturalistic poetry, a gorgeous palate of violet and turquoise, Rousseau’s waking wet dream. Tom began the long desert trek, the isolating span of perpetual morning hope invigorated his dehydrated, dirt worn body. Tied fastly to his upper torso with thick well aged rope, Tom's supplies were tightly secured within the canvas tent, a boy's rucksack trekking through nature with a sheepish knowledge of the impending supper call. The perimeter of mountains lay dormant in the distance, a panoramic heard of beady eyed giants bowing in meditation. Flashes of a past existence raced through Tom's mind, poison tipped spears of regret flying savagely by with no strength left for repulsion. The life of the meek should be cut down and annihilated. Our precious realm of modern materialism leeched of its foundation and replaced with well-planned Ebola holidays. The cold steel barrel of Tom's gun pressed firmly against his withering stomach, an isolated reaper of redemption. Tom reached the foot of the mountainside, Death Valley's heart stretching in vast oppressive glory sneaking closely behind him. Tom removed his tent and extracted a single two centimeter square of nicely formulated acid and pressed it gingerly against his tongue while cracking open a long since warmed beer, draining it quickly. Realigning his makeshift hiking pack, Tom began the long trek toward the mountain’s jagged peak.

Fiona entered the small, somewhat fancy Italian joint and slowly scanned the dining room. She spotted Tom sitting at a corner table adjacent to the inquiring hostess. “We shouldn’t be long,” Fiona told the overly cheery hostess as she made her way to the empty seat across from Tom. He was studying the menu intensely, nose pressed against the paper, rimmed reading glasses resting on the bridge of his nose. Tom looked up, his frown quickly transitioning to a beaming, overemphasized smile. “Ah Fiona, you look stunning!” Tom said with all honesty. “Thank you Tom, how was your day, how was work?”

“Productive”, Tom replied without pause. “We should talk Tom.” The waitress placed small plates of artichokes with a practiced finesse in front of them, tiny ramekins of aioli, pith-less lemon wedges adorning each plate’s edge. Fiona despised artichokes. Out of any food imaginable, Tom had to order the one she’d expressed to him a considerable number of times her distaste for. Tom plucked a leaf from his artichoke and dipped it contentedly into his little ramekin. “I’m lost, and a lookin, for my baby,” Sam Cooke played quietly from a speaker above. “I don’t think this is working Tom,” Fiona said in a soft, near whisper. Tom stopped chewing and looked up, a shocked expression on his face. He stared achingly at Fiona, racking his brain for an appropriate response. Something to reverse the haunting words he’d barely registered from Fiona’s lips. Tom choked on his food and began coughing loudly while stuttering nonsensical apologies and coercive phrases. “How can I change, what happened? Please I’ll do anything!” Tom said, manic and distraught. “I need some time for myself Tom, to figure out my life, my future,” Fiona replied with sincerity. “I’m sorry Tom, but that’s how I’ve felt for a long time. I’m so sorry.” Fiona rose from her chair. “Take care of yourself Tom.” Fiona turned and walked out of the restaurant. Sam Cooke continued playing softly from above.

Tom set up his tent on a small landing resting precariously against the mountains side, paranoid and skittish. Tiny voices seemed to echo softly to his right, indecipherable whispers, incoherent conversations between lost spirits. Tom thought of his cubicle, his tiny chamber of truth, that sweet glowing screen of existential purpose, an imaginary fixture of American idealism. “I’ve been a fool for far too long,” Tom thought, lonely and afraid. The whispers droned on, unheard messages lost in time.

Fiona drove freely feeling relaxed and relieved. Her newly instilled sense of freedom gripped her with hope. She clung to a tiny, distant light of peace that melted into a pool of anxiety as she looked down at her dress and remembered the innocent perpetuation of imprisonment growing within. Fiona sighed and smiled weakly. “Whatever happens, happens,” she thought, the speck of light rising, her anxiety solidifying to determination. Fiona sat peacefully on her couch watching the news and gently stroking Horace. The old cat purred, ears erect with closed relaxed eyes.

Tom sat in his car peering madly at Fiona’s apartment. His anger was boiling over, a slow burn witch trial of hate. He’d fastened a rope to the rim of a thick plastic bag. He stepped out of the car placing thin, disposable hospital gloves on his trembling hands. Fiona spread her arms yawning fervently and softly closed her eyes. “Good night Horace.” Fiona smiled and scratched the cat’s head. Horace did a quick shake and wandered off the couch. Fiona removed her dress and climbed into bed, foregoing a night gown. The sheets felt cool and soothing on her body, a curvy, delicate shape embodying youth. “It won’t be like this for much longer,” Fiona thought, easing into a restful sleep. Tom slid his key silently into the lock. Fiona hadn’t asked for her key back, and Tom sure as hell didn’t mention he had it. He opened the door, crept silently through the entrance and entered Fiona’s kitchen. Tom grabbed a kitchen knife from the magnetic strip on the wall. “Just in case,” he thought. He carefully opened Fiona’s door and sat at the foot of her bed.

Tom’s heart sank and he began quietly sobbing. He regained composure and untied the rope surrounding the plastic bag. Tom watched Fiona breathe, leaning in closely to feel her warmth. Tom slowly placed the bag over Fiona’s head and took a deep breath. He lowered the bag fast and clean on Fiona’s head, tightening the rope around her petit neck. Fiona’s eyes flashed opened and she screamed violently, thrashing and shaking her head. She tried to breath but the air was lost. She was trapped in a vacuum without hope. Tom struggled to hold the bag in place, forgetting the kitchen knife he’d placed on the night stand. Fiona flung her arms feeling for something to grab and felt the blade of the knife, squeezing it and feeling its sharp bite dig deep into her hand. A steady stream of blood flowed from her wound as she moved toward the handle and struggled to grip the blood slick knife as she swiftly slashed at Tom’s waist. Tom screamed and covered his mouth, all too aware of the thin walled apartment, intimate flashback of sweat and pounding walls ringing like fire alarms in his head. He squeezed Fiona’s wrist tightly. He grabbed the knife from her slick red hand and held it high. Tom brought the knife down with wild unhinged force, once, twice, Tom eventually lost count. Tom was dripping with deep burgundy blood, his eyes stinging from Fiona’s spurting wounds. She coughed and sprayed the plastic bag red as blood filled her lungs and her vision faded to white, fuzzy nothingness. Fiona saw her mother’s face, kind and wise without a glimmer of hate. Her eyes were clear and clean, heavenly blue lights of the past. Through the stained plastic bag, unbeknownst to Tom, Fiona smiled in acceptance as death took her hand and guided her to tranquility. Tom assessed Fiona’s body and cried again before rubbing his hands together and regaining thought. He surveyed the damage; stained sheets, lifeless body. “It’s been to hard livin, but I’m afraid to die. Cuz I don’t know what’s up there, beyond the sky,” he sang quietly. Tom had work to do.

Tom reached the mountain’s peak just as the afternoon heat simmered and baked the impenetrable Death Valley air. The mountain leveled off into a long stretch of cracked earth. He hiked with fractured purpose along the endless plateau and set up his tent, retrieving the sheets of acid and remaining beer. Tom stared at the sheets for several minutes, eyes blurring and watering. He folded the sheets in a precise sluggish fashion into a thin two by four inch rectangle. He placed the sheets in his mouth and allowed them to dissolve, the onslaught of schizophrenic nightmares soaking into his bloodstream. Tom had taken two hundred ninety nine tabs, the event horizon loomed in the distance. He drained the last of his beer and stepped from his tent into the sun, kneeling, then sitting legs crossed, tipsy eyes focused ahead. For two hours Tom sat, sweating bullets and waiting for change. The stark hues of ragged desert terrain merged and coalesced with the sun. Tom shook violently and rubbed his shoulders. Voices yelled from plains of altered existence, a deer walked by on its hind legs and smiled at Tom before grimacing and screaming in horror, its face melting and collecting in beads on the desert floor. An old, dusty computer lay on the brink of the plateau, the keyboard tapping frantically as if possessed by demons of futurist design. A woman approached, an initial speck on the limitless plateau. She moved carefully toward Tom. Her figure morphed through space and stood before him. It was Fiona. She looked beautiful, a young unscathed soul depicting god’s presence and alleviating humanities’ sorrow with still, knowing exemption. Fiona smiled at Tom as he looked into Fiona’s eyes. He began weeping, tears running down his cheeks. A river of reticent shame. “I’m so sorry,” he whispered, repeating the words until they lost all meaning. Fiona approached and rested her palm on Tom’s head. She nodded to him, a radiant light of being forever lost in the destructive jealousy of ego. Tom felt a warm sensation on his chest. He looked down and saw several deep, blazing red holes straight through his chest. Blood pooled and replaced his dusty shirt with a bright fluid glow, drenching his body with mindless hostility. Tom coughed and sputtered as blood poured from his mouth. He gasped for breath as he choked and convulsed. Fiona turned and glided away serenely down the sneaking plateau. Tom lifted an arm, “don’t go,” he anemically choked out. Those were his final words. Tom heard a shot and felt a white hot pain of deterioration as he was ripped from consciousness toward a dark, endless black hole of nothing.

A pair of long haul hikers discovered Tom’s body several weeks later. His skin was decayed, flies buzzing aimlessly around his ruined face, distorted and mangled by a blast to the forehead. His chest was riddled with holes. A pungent stench of death permeated the suffocating desert air. A gun was held loosely in Tom’s left palm, shells piled closely together. In his right hand, Tom held a cracked tumbler with a picture of Charlie Chaplin gracing its side.

The sun slipped away and the moon took hold of the desolate desert landscape. A ceaseless procession of shifting light illuminating the world and preserving life’s beauty. “These things I’ll be until I die,” The repeating chorus crackled through Tom’s abandoned junkyard speakers. Stillness prevailed and society rambled on nervously, waking from peaceful slumber to a deep red sky.
